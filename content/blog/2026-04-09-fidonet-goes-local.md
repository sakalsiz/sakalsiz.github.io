FidoNet was built in America. Zone 1, North America. Zone 2,
Europe. Zone 3, Australia. And so on. The protocol was open. The
software was available. The idea was contagious.

But the phone bills were {lightred}brutal{/}.

Calling your FidoNet hub in another city cost real money. Calling
across countries cost serious money. Calling across continents was
out of the question for most hobbyists. The solution was obvious:
{white}build your own network, locally{/}.

## The national networks

Across the world, BBS communities took the FidoNet technology
stack and created independent networks that ran on {yellow}local
phone calls{/}. Same protocols. Same software. Same packet format.
Different node numbering, different echoes, different languages.

Here are some of the most notable:

## Turkey: HitNet

{yellow}HitNet{/} was Turkey's homegrown FidoNet-compatible network.
It connected Turkish BBSes across Istanbul, Ankara, Izmir, and
other cities. The echoes were in {lightcyan}Turkish{/}. The culture
was local. The SysOps knew each other by voice because Turkey's
BBS scene was small enough that everyone eventually met at a
gathering or a computer fair.

HitNet ran on the same mailer and tosser software as FidoNet
(Front Door, FastEcho, etc.) but with its own addressing space.
Messages stayed inside Turkey. Phone calls stayed inside Turkey.
A local call in Istanbul cost almost nothing. That was the whole
point.

Some HitNet nodes also had FidoNet addresses and acted as
{lightgreen}gateways{/}, forwarding selected international echoes
into HitNet and Turkish echoes back out to the wider FidoNet.
Two networks, bridged at a few points.

## Russia

Russia had a growing BBS scene through the early 1990s despite
unreliable phone infrastructure. That made networking harder and
the community tighter.

Russian BBS culture had a distinctive flavor: heavy {lightcyan}
demoscene{/} influence, {lightcyan}ANSI art{/} competitions, and a
tradition of long-form writing in message echoes that carried over
into early Russian internet culture.

## Germany: FidoNet Zone 2 powerhouse

Germany did not need a separate network. It was so deeply embedded
in FidoNet Zone 2 that it {white}was{/} the network in many ways.
German SysOps ran some of the most organized hubs in Europe.
{yellow}MausNet{/} was a parallel German-language network that ran
alongside FidoNet, focused on technical discussion. Some German
nodes were on both.

The German BBS scene was also home to {lightgreen}CrossPoint{/},
the most popular offline reader in Europe, written by
{white}Peter Mandrella{/}. CrossPoint supported FidoNet, MausNet,
USENET, and internet email all in one interface.

## Brazil: RBT and Fido Zone 4

Brazil was Zone 4 in FidoNet's official numbering but also spawned
local networks. {yellow}RBT{/} (Rede Brasileira de TeleInformatica) was
one of the earliest. Brazilian BBS culture was huge. By the mid-
1990s Brazil had one of the largest BBS communities outside the
United States, driven by affordable local phone calls and a young,
tech-curious population.

Brazilian echoes covered everything from {lightcyan}programming{/}
to {lightcyan}music{/} to {lightcyan}politics{/}. Some of the
earliest Portuguese-language internet culture traces back to
Brazilian BBS message bases.

## Australia: Zone 3

Australia ran FidoNet Zone 3 but distance was the enemy. A "local
call" from Sydney to Melbourne was long-distance. Australian SysOps
organized into tight regional nets to keep costs down. The culture
was resourceful. Australian BBSes were early adopters of {yellow}
ISDN{/} and {yellow}X.25 packet switching{/} as alternatives to
expensive analog long-distance.

## South Africa: Zone 5

Zone 5 covered Africa, and in practice that meant {yellow}South
Africa{/}. South African BBSes connected through Johannesburg hubs.
International connectivity was expensive and slow. The local scene
was self-contained and fiercely independent.

## Japan: private networks

Japan's BBS tradition was separate from FidoNet entirely. Systems
like {yellow}PC-VAN{/} and {yellow}NIFTY-Serve{/} were commercial
networks run by NEC and Fujitsu (via Nifty Corporation, a joint
venture with Nissho Iwai) respectively. They were closer to
CompuServe than to FidoNet. But smaller hobbyist boards existed
too, running Japanese-language message bases with {lightcyan}Shift-
JIS encoding{/} and full-width characters.

The {lightgreen}kaomoji{/} emoticon tradition (^_^) came from these
systems, not from western BBSes.

## The pattern

Every country that had modems and phone lines eventually produced
the same thing: a small group of hobbyists who wanted to talk to
each other, share files, and build community. The technology was
the same everywhere. The protocol was the same. What differed was
the {white}language{/}, the {white}culture{/}, and the {white}phone
rates{/}.

FidoNet's greatest achievement was not connecting 30,000 nodes.
It was proving that {yellow}the model worked everywhere{/}. Any
group of people with modems could build a network. You did not
need permission. You did not need funding. You needed a phone line,
a copy of Front Door, and the desire to connect.

The national clones were not clones at all. They were {green}
independent reinventions of the same idea{/}, happening in parallel,
in every language, on every continent.
