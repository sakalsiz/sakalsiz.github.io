If you think a {white}global threaded forum{/} that runs on
volunteer-operated nodes with no central server is a recent idea,
let me tell you about {yellow}EchoMail{/}.

EchoMail was FidoNet's distributed conference system. Each
conference was called an {lightcyan}echo{/}, because messages
{lightgreen}echoed{/} from one BBS to the next. You posted a
message on your local BBS. Overnight your BBS handed the message
to its uplink. The uplink handed it to its uplinks. By the
following morning, your message had hopped across the world.

## How an echo worked

Each echo had a {white}moderator{/} who set the rules and resolved
disputes. Each echo had a {white}tag{/} like:

 {yellow}DOORS.GAMES{/}        Discussion of BBS door games
 {yellow}PROGRAMMING.C{/}      C language Q and A
 {yellow}R20_FOR_SALE{/}       For-sale ads in FidoNet Region 20
 {yellow}SCIENCE{/}            Hard science discussion
 {yellow}CHATTER{/}            General BBS chatter

Subscribing to an echo meant {white}requesting{/} it from your
uplink. The uplink would mark your node as a downstream subscriber.
Future messages in that echo would flow to you. Reply in the echo,
and your reply flowed back upstream and out to everyone else.

## Tossing and scanning

Mail came in as compressed packet files (`.PKT` inside `.ZIP`).
A piece of software called a {white}tosser{/} unpacked the packet,
figured out which echoes the messages belonged to, and wrote them
into the appropriate {lightcyan}message base{/}. Popular tossers:
FastEcho, Squish, Crashmail, GEcho.

Reading was done in a {white}reader{/} or directly in the BBS
message viewer. Writing was done in the same viewer. The cycle
ran every night.

## The famous echoes

Some echoes became legendary. {lightcyan}IBMPROG{/} was a graveyard
of arguments about C++ vs Pascal. {lightgreen}NET.HISTORY{/} (no
relation to Internet history) collected stories of the network's
early years. {yellow}HOLYWAR{/} existed for the explicit purpose of
having pointless flame wars in one place so other echoes could stay
civil.

## Compared to USENET

USENET did the same thing over the {white}internet{/}, with NNTP
and a more permissive flood-fill propagation. EchoMail did it
over {white}modems{/}, with a stricter star-and-hub topology and
a moderator system that actually meant something.

You could read both. Many people did. They felt different. EchoMail
was {gray}slower{/} and {gray}smaller{/} and {gray}closer{/}.
