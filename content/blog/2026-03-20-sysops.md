Every BBS had a {yellow}SysOp{/}. System Operator. The person who
owned the hardware, paid the phone bill, set the rules, and stayed
up too late maintaining the thing.

SysOps were {white}local celebrities{/} in their scenes. Their
boards had personalities because they had personalities. The board
was an expression of who they were.

## What they actually did

A typical SysOp day:

 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Read overnight logs{/} to see what users
    did, who logged in, who got disconnected, what files were
    downloaded.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Curate the file area{/}: organize new
    uploads into the right directories, write descriptions,
    delete junk.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Moderate messages{/}: read every public
    post, kick out the trolls, pin good threads.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Answer netmail{/} from users with technical
    problems, account requests, or just wanting to chat.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Maintain hardware{/}. Defrag the drive.
    Run ScanDisk. Replace the modem when it died. The BBS was on
    a {gray}386 in the spare bedroom{/} and it ran 24/7. Things
    broke.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Update the menus{/} with new ANSI art.
    Most SysOps fancied themselves designers and would tweak the
    welcome screen monthly.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Pay the phone bill{/}. Cry quietly.

## The hardware

A respectable SysOp setup in {yellow}1993{/}:

 {green}▓▓{/} {white}486DX2/66{/} with 16 MB RAM
 {green}▓▓{/} {white}540 MB hard drive{/} (this was a lot)
 {green}▓▓{/} {white}USR Sportster 14.4{/} or 28.8 modem
 {green}▓▓{/} A second phone line just for the BBS, which the
    family was not allowed to pick up
 {green}▓▓{/} A monitor that stayed on amber {gray}24/7{/} showing
    the call log
 {green}▓▓{/} {white}DOS 6.22{/} with DESQview to multitask the
    mailer, the BBS, and the file area maintenance utilities

Total cost in 1993 dollars: around $3000. In current dollars: about
$6000. Plus the phone line, plus long-distance charges if you were
on FidoNet.

## Why people did it

Three reasons, usually:

 {yellow}1.{/} {white}Status{/}. Running a board with 200 active
    users meant being known. Inside your scene you were somebody.
 {yellow}2.{/} {white}Curation{/}. You got to shape the board's
    culture, decide what was talked about, what art ran on the
    welcome screen, what files were on offer. It was {lightgreen}
    creative work{/} disguised as system administration.
 {yellow}3.{/} {white}Community{/}. You knew your callers by name.
    They knew you. Some of them became real-life friends. SysOp
    meets ({lightcyan}face-to-face gatherings{/}) were a regular
    thing.

When the web arrived, most SysOps {gray}did not transition{/} to
running websites. The thing they loved was the small, local,
known-by-name community. The web killed that. A lot of them just
took the BBS down, sent a final {green}NO CARRIER{/}, and went
back to having weekends.
