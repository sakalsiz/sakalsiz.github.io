In {yellow}December 1993{/}, a small Texas company called
{white}id Software{/} uploaded a file to Software Creations BBS in
Massachusetts and an FTP server at the University of Wisconsin-
Madison. Within twenty-four hours it was on every BBS in North
America. The download traffic at UW-Madison was so massive it
crashed the network. Universities started {lightred}banning DOOM's
multiplayer{/} from their LANs because it was eating all the
bandwidth.

The file was {lightcyan}DOOM1_0.ZIP{/}.

## How shareware actually worked

The model was simple: {white}give away enough of the game to
hook them, charge for the rest{/}.

DOOM had {lightgreen}three episodes{/}. Episode 1, "Knee-Deep in
the Dead," was completely free. You could play it forever. You
could share it with friends. You were {white}encouraged{/} to share
it. You could not, however, play episodes 2 and 3 unless you sent
{lightcyan}$40 to id Software{/} and they mailed you the registered
disks.

The genius of this model was that the {yellow}distribution channel
cost id nothing{/}. Tens of thousands of SysOps and bulletin board
operators happily hosted the shareware version because it brought
callers to their boards. id paid no shipping, no retail markup, no
shelf space, no marketing. People {green}voluntarily mass-distributed{/}
their game.

A partial list of programs that reached millions of users via BBS
shareware before retail:

 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}DOOM{/} - id Software, 1993. Genre-defining.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Wolfenstein 3D{/} - id Software, 1992.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Commander Keen{/} - id Software, 1990-91.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Duke Nukem{/} - Apogee Software, 1991.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Jill of the Jungle{/} - Epic MegaGames, 1992.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Tyrian{/} - Eclipse Software / Epic MegaGames, 1995.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}WinZip{/} - Nico Mak Computing.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}McAfee VirusScan{/} - the OG free antivirus.
 {lightcyan}▓▓{/} {white}Paint Shop Pro{/} - JASC.

These programs reached enormous user bases without a single retail
deal. The BBS file areas were the {yellow}distribution layer{/}.

## What it taught the industry

Shareware demonstrated three things that the software industry was
slow to learn:

 {lightgreen}1.{/} {white}Free demos work{/}. Letting people use
    your software before they buy it does not destroy sales. It
    drives them.
 {lightgreen}2.{/} {white}Distribution can be free{/}. If your
    users want to share your software, you can let them, and that
    becomes your marketing budget.
 {lightgreen}3.{/} {white}Direct-to-consumer beats retail{/}. id
    sold registered DOOM through the mail. Apogee sold via order
    forms in their games. They kept {lightcyan}most of the revenue{/}.
    Retail boxed games kept maybe 30%.

## Why it matters now

Every {yellow}freemium app{/} ever shipped is a descendant of
1993 shareware. {white}Steam{/} sells episodic content. {white}Apple's
App Store{/} hosts free downloads with paid unlocks. {white}Spotify{/}
runs on a "free tier with paid features" model that DOOM
established three decades earlier.

The BBS scene invented online software distribution. Then the web
arrived and {gray}forgot to credit them{/}.
